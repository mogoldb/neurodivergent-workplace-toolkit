# Meeting Structure

Guidelines for preparing, participating in, and following up on meetings — especially for neurodivergent professionals who may find the unstructured, fast-paced nature of meetings cognitively demanding.

---

## Why Meetings Are Hard

Meetings require simultaneous processing: listening, understanding, formulating responses, tracking who has spoken, managing turn-taking norms, and knowing when to interject. They often have implicit agendas, unclear roles, and undefined success criteria. For many neurodivergent people, the social and sensory load of a meeting competes directly with the cognitive work of actually contributing.

This guide provides structure to reduce that load.

---

## Before the Meeting — Preparation

**Know your role before you walk in.** Are you:
- A decision-maker (expected to approve or decide things)?
- A contributor (expected to share information or opinions)?
- An observer (there to learn or stay informed)?
- A facilitator (running the meeting)?

Your role determines what you need to prepare. A contributor needs talking points. A decision-maker needs context. An observer needs to know what they're watching for.

**Prepare 2-3 key points** in advance. Write them down. If you want to make sure you say something, write it as a full sentence you can refer to. You don't need to memorize it.

**Identify your asks.** What do you need from others in this meeting? Approval? Information? A decision? Know this before you go in.

**Decode the agenda.** Each agenda item usually has a real purpose beneath the label:
- "Updates" = what's in flight, who's blocked, what's changed
- "Discussion" = a decision needs to be made, or a problem needs solving
- "Review" = get feedback or approval
- "AOB / Any other business" = often used for issues that didn't fit elsewhere — have space for surprises

**Questions to prepare:** Write down things you need clarified or decided. Having them written means you won't forget them when put on the spot.

---

## During the Meeting

**Take notes for yourself, not just the room.** Even if someone else is note-taking, write your own: action items for you, decisions that affect you, questions you still have.

**Track your action items in real time.** When someone assigns you a task, write: Task / What / Deadline. Don't rely on remembering it.

**On speaking up:**
- If you're unsure when to speak, wait for a natural pause, then: "I want to add something to [topic]..." 
- If the conversation moves on before you can speak, it's okay to say "Can we go back to [X] for a second?"
- If you're called on unexpectedly, it's okay to say "Let me think for a second" before answering.

**On asking for clarity:** "Just want to make sure I understand — are you saying X?" is always appropriate in a meeting. It helps everyone.

**Watch for implicit asks:** If someone looks at you when describing a problem, they may be asking you to respond or commit. If in doubt: "Is that something you need from me?"

**On going off-topic:** Meetings often drift. If this is your meeting: "Let me note that for the parking lot and bring us back to [agenda item]." If not your meeting: note the tangent, see if it gets resolved, raise it again at the end if needed.

---

## After the Meeting

**Within 24 hours:**
- Write up or confirm your action items (task / owner / deadline)
- Send a summary if you were facilitating, or if there's risk of misremembered decisions
- If you weren't sure what you were assigned, follow up now

**Follow-up summary format:**
```
Decisions made:
- [Decision 1]
- [Decision 2]

Action items:
- [Name]: [Task] by [Date]
- [Name]: [Task] by [Date]

Open questions:
- [Question that wasn't resolved]

Next steps / next meeting: [Date / topic]
```

**If you're blocked on something from the meeting:** surface it the same day, not the day before the deadline.

---

## Preparation Shortcut — 5 Questions

When you don't have time for full prep, answer these five before walking in:

1. What is this meeting actually for?
2. What do I need to say?
3. What do I need to find out?
4. What decisions might I be asked to make?
5. What are my action items likely to be?

Five minutes with these five questions is usually enough to walk in prepared.
