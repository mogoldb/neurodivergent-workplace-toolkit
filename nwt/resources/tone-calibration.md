# Tone Calibration

Guidelines for assessing and adjusting message tone — especially for neurodivergent communicators whose natural directness or precision can sometimes read differently than intended.

---

## Why Tone Is Tricky

Neurodivergent communication often defaults to directness, precision, and efficiency. These are genuine strengths — but in workplaces with unspoken tone norms, a technically clear message can land as curt, cold, or even aggressive, especially in writing where facial expression and vocal tone are absent.

The goal is not to suppress directness. It's to add the small signals that help readers receive the message as intended.

---

## Professional Tone Levels

**Formal** — use for: external stakeholders, executives, legal/compliance contexts, first contact with someone you don't know
- Full sentences, no contractions
- No slang or colloquialisms
- Polite openers and closers
- Example: "I hope this message finds you well. I am writing to follow up on..."

**Standard** — use for: cross-team communication, managers (unless you have a casual relationship), written records that may be shared
- Contractions fine ("I'm", "we'll")
- Direct, professional
- Brief warmth but not overly casual
- Example: "Hey Sarah, following up on the API design review — can you confirm the timeline?"

**Casual** — use for: direct teammates you work with daily, Slack DMs in established relationships, informal status updates
- Contractions, shorthand, emoji if that's your team's norm
- Get to the point quickly
- Example: "quick q — did the deploy go through or are we waiting on infra?"

**When in doubt:** default to Standard. You can always loosen tone later; it's harder to recover from being seen as too casual in a formal context.

## Tone Markers to Add

These small additions shift how a direct message is received without changing its substance:

**Acknowledge effort or context before correcting:**
- Instead of: "This approach has a problem."
- Try: "I can see the thinking here — one concern I want to flag..."

**Name the intent of a question:**
- Instead of: "Why did you do it this way?"
- Try: "Curious about the reasoning — was there a constraint I'm missing?"

**Soften hard deadlines with brief context:**
- Instead of: "I need this by Thursday."
- Try: "I need this by Thursday — we're presenting Friday morning."

**Signal that criticism is collaborative:**
- "I want to flag a concern" vs. stating the concern bluntly
- "Worth discussing" vs. "this is wrong"

## Red Flags — Signs a Message May Land Badly

**Content red flags:**
- "I already explained this" — sounds condescending even if factually accurate
- "Obviously" / "clearly" / "as I mentioned" — can sound dismissive
- Starting with "Actually" — often reads as a correction or contradiction
- Passive voice to avoid blame: "Mistakes were made" — often comes across as evasive
- Assuming bad intent: "You ignored my message" vs. "I wanted to follow up in case it got buried"

**Formatting red flags:**
- ALL CAPS for emphasis (reads as shouting)
- Multiple exclamation marks!!!! (reads as sarcasm or aggression in professional contexts)
- No greeting at all for an email (reads as cold or abrupt)
- Very long blocks of text with no paragraph breaks (hard to read, can feel overwhelming)

**Missing signals:**
- No acknowledgment that the reader might have constraints you don't know about
- No "thank you" or close in a formal request
- No "let me know if you have questions" when sharing something complex

---

## Neurodivergent Directness Is Often Appropriate

Many workplaces increasingly value direct, clear communication. The patterns that feel "too blunt" in one context are the preferred style in another — engineering teams, for example, often prefer directness over diplomatic hedging.

Read your specific context:
- What's the norm on your team?
- What's your established relationship with this person?
- Is this a high-stakes message (performance, conflict, formal record) or a routine one?

When you match tone to context, you preserve your directness where it works and add cushioning only where it's needed.
