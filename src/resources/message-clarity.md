# Message Clarity Rules

Guidelines for analyzing and improving message clarity, especially for neurodivergent communication styles that tend toward precision, completeness, and directness.

---

## Core Structure Principles

**Lead with the ask, not the context.**
Most messages bury the request at the end after paragraphs of background. Put the main point or ask in the first sentence. Readers scan — make it impossible to miss.

**One message, one main point.**
Combining multiple unrelated asks in one message leads to partial responses. If you have three separate things to communicate, consider whether they warrant separate messages.

**Define the call to action explicitly.**
"Let me know your thoughts" is too vague. "Can you approve this by Thursday?" is clear. Every message that requires action should have a named action and, if relevant, a deadline.

**State assumptions out loud.**
Neurodivergent communicators often check assumptions explicitly ("I'm assuming this means X — is that right?"). This is a strength, not a weakness. It prevents rework and misalignment.

---

## Clarity Checklist

Before sending, verify:

- [ ] Is the main ask in the first 1-2 sentences?
- [ ] Have you defined acronyms on first use?
- [ ] Is "you" specific — do you name the person when addressing them?
- [ ] Is the timeline explicit ("by Friday EOD", not "soon")?
- [ ] Have you stated what you need vs. what you're sharing as context?
- [ ] Is the message the right length — neither too sparse to understand nor too long to read?
- [ ] Would a reader know what to do next after reading this?

---

## Common Clarity Problems

**Buried ask:** The request appears at the end, after several paragraphs of context. Readers may stop reading before they reach it.

**Assumed context:** The writer knows why this matters, but the reader doesn't. Always explain the "why" briefly, especially cross-team.

**Vague timelines:** "ASAP", "when you get a chance", "soon" all mean different things to different people. Use specific dates or acknowledge the ambiguity.

**Unclear pronouns:** "They said it should be done" — who is "they"? Name people and systems explicitly.

**Softened asks:** "I was wondering if maybe you could possibly..." obscures what you actually need. Directness is not rudeness.

**Missing "what I need from you":** If you want a decision, say you want a decision. If you want information, ask for that specifically.

---

## Length Guidelines

- **Slack message / quick question:** 1-3 sentences. If it needs more, use a thread or email.
- **Email update:** 3-5 short paragraphs max. Use headers or bullets if covering multiple points.
- **Formal doc or spec:** Length as needed, but lead with an executive summary.

---

## When Neurodivergent Style Is Actually Clearer

Neurodivergent communication often includes patterns that are objectively more precise:

- Explicit about assumptions ("I'm assuming X")
- Literal and specific ("I need this by 3pm Thursday", not "soon")
- Complete — includes all relevant context upfront
- Questions clarifications rather than guessing

These patterns often produce better outcomes than vague "polished" messages. The goal of this toolkit is not to make messages sound more neurotypical — it's to ensure the right information lands clearly.
